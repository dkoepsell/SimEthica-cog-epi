Moral Dynamics of Nine Normative Regimes:
An Empirical Study Using SimEthica

David R. Koepsell, J.D., Ph.D.
Department of Philosophy, Texas A&M University
SimEthica Platform — Geometry of the Good Research Program — 2025
Working Paper — Not for citation without permission
Abstract

This report presents findings from a series of agent-based simulation experiments using SimEthica, a platform developed to operationalize core concepts from the Geometry of the Good and the Architecture of Social Being research programs. Nine distinct normative regimes were simulated across generation ranges of 425 to 1,755 generations, yielding approximately 900 data records, over 700,000 cumulative obligation transactions, and six measured welfare axes per generation. The central finding is that aggregate moral energy converges across structurally opposed regimes and is therefore insufficient as a normative criterion. A four-dimensional welfare space — comprising moral energy, floor energy (minimum agent welfare), epistemic agency, and contradiction debt — is necessary and sufficient to differentiate regime types. Five distinct debt trajectory signatures are identified as regime fingerprints. The rupture condition is specified as a structural defeat of the repair mechanism rather than a debt-level threshold. An extended 1,755-generation communitarian run reveals a demographic self-purging dynamic absent from shorter runs. Recommendations for parameter configurations maximizing long-run equilibrium and normative welfare are provided, alongside a detailed prospectus for future experimental design.

1. Introduction

SimEthica is an agent-based simulation platform in which populations of autonomous agents operate under one of nine moral-political regimes, forming and fulfilling (or failing to fulfill) obligations with one another, accumulating or clearing contradiction debt, and building or eroding interpersonal trust. The platform operationalizes a cluster of theoretical concepts from the author’s ongoing research program in structural moral ontology, particularly the notions of moral energy, contradiction debt, epistemic agency, and obligation fulfillment as developed in The Geometry of the Good (2025) and A Structural Ontology of the Law.

The nine regimes simulated correspond to ideal-typical forms of moral-political organization found in the normative philosophy literature: ∅ Anarchist, △ Hierarchical, ⟲ Reciprocal, ⊜ Egalitarian, ⬡ Communitarian, ∑ Utilitarian, ⇌ Contractarian, ⚡ Authoritarian, and ✦ Pluralist. Each regime modifies the core simulation mechanics — repair probability, denial probability, belief-vector dynamics, obligation formation thresholds — in ways that reflect the structural features of the corresponding normative theory.

The experimental design contains a significant confound that must be disclosed at the outset: three regimes (anarchist, hierarchical, reciprocal) were run without external pressure, while six (egalitarian, communitarian, utilitarian, contractarian, authoritarian, pluralist) were run under a pressure ramp from approximately 0.31 to 1.0. This asymmetry means that cross-regime comparisons partly reflect pressure-response differences rather than pure regime-type differences. All findings involving pressured regimes should be understood as findings about those regimes under those pressure conditions, not about the regimes as such. Resolving this confound is the primary methodological priority for future work.

2. The SimEthica Platform

Each agent in SimEthica maintains six core state variables: moral energy (0–1), contradiction debt (0+), average trust (0–1), epistemic autonomy (0–1), cognitive agency (0–1), and rank (0–1). Agents move through a three-dimensional world space, forming obligations when they come within the obligation radius of compatible partners. Each obligation resolves each step as fulfilled (with probability rP), denied (with probability dP), or expired (if its age exceeds the obligation life parameter).

Fulfilled obligations increase trust and moral energy for the issuer and reduce contradiction debt. Denied obligations increase contradiction debt and reduce trust. Expired obligations increase contradiction debt without the social consequence of an active denial. The regime parameter modifies rP and dP multipliers and governs belief-vector dynamics, group cohesion, rank effects, consent thresholds, and epistemic variance.

External pressure (extPressure, 0–1) drives agents’ belief vectors toward an official vector and suppresses epistemic autonomy. It is ramped using the pressureRamp parameter. Reproduction occurs when two agents whose belief vectors satisfy the beliefSimilarityReq threshold have sufficient moral energy and trust; death occurs when agents exceed the deathThresh in contradiction debt.

4. Results

4.1 Moral Energy: Convergence and Its Implications

The most striking finding across all nine regimes is the convergence of aggregate moral energy toward values between 97% and 100% at equilibrium (Figure 2). Contractarian achieves 100.0%; communitarian 100.0%; anarchist 100.0%; authoritarian 99.2%; pluralist 98.5%. Regimes that are structurally maximally opposed — anarchist and authoritarian, contractarian and utilitarian — arrive at indistinguishable aggregate energy figures while differing radically on every other welfare metric.

Figure 2. Moral energy trajectories (solid lines) and minimum agent energy (dashed lines) across all nine regimes on a unified 0–1,020 generation axis. Note energy convergence near ceiling for all regimes. The dashed minimum lines reveal the distribution beneath the aggregate: hierarchical’s minimum hits zero at generation 50 and never recovers; contractarian’s minimum reaches 1.000. The gap between solid and dashed lines is the primary welfare differentiator. Shaded phase bands mark early (green), mid (amber), and late (blue) periods.

This convergence establishes that aggregate moral energy is a necessary but nowhere near sufficient criterion for normative evaluation. A regime that achieves 99% energy through death-cycling of low-energy agents (authoritarian) is not in the same moral condition as one that achieves it through voluntary cooperation (anarchist), even though both register the same aggregate value. The framework requires additional axes.

4.2 The Floor: Rawlsian Differentiation

Minimum agent energy — the worst-off agent at each generation — is the single sharpest differentiator across all nine regimes (Figure 3). Hierarchical’s floor hits zero at generation 50 and remains there for the full 485-generation run. A permanent underclass exists throughout. Contractarian reaches a floor of 100% through a mechanism that eliminates moral activity entirely. All other regimes maintain nonzero floors, with anarchist stabilizing around 93% and communitarian oscillating between 75% and 90%.

Figure 3. Minimum agent energy (floor) trajectories. Hierarchical (orange) is categorically distinct: permanent zero floor from generation 50. Contractarian (teal) achieves 100% floor through system closure. All other regimes maintain positive floors. The Rawlsian ordering by floor welfare places anarchist first among active regimes, followed by communitarian, with hierarchical uniquely failing the difference principle.

The Rawlsian significance is direct: an agent choosing a regime behind a veil of ignorance should prefer anarchist unconditionally over hierarchical. The floor difference is not marginal but categorical. Hierarchy fails the difference principle not by distributing unequally but by permanently eliminating the floor condition for a structural subpopulation.

4.3 Trust: Dissociability from Energy

Trust and moral energy are dissociable. Six regimes achieve near-maximal energy with trust collapsed to near zero (Figure 4). Only anarchist and communitarian (early phases) achieve high trust alongside high energy. This finding undermines any theory that treats trust as constitutive of moral health. Trust is a pathway variable: high trust enables certain efficient routes to moral energy and has better distributional properties, but energy can be produced through enforcement, freezing, or demographic mechanisms in its absence.

Figure 4. Average trust trajectories. Anarchist (pink) builds trust monotonically through fulfilled voluntary obligations. Communitarian (purple) peaks at 82.3% (generation 145) then declines as pressure overwhelms cohesion, with partial recovery in the extended run as mortality removes low-trust agents. All other pressured regimes collapse to near-zero by generation 150. The unpressured hierarchical regime (orange) also collapses despite no external pressure, revealing trust suppression as a structural feature of rank mechanics.

4.4 The Debt Typology

Contradiction debt trajectories form five distinct shapes across the nine regimes, each causally explicable from the regime mechanics (Figure 5). This typology is the most diagnostically useful single framework to emerge from the dataset.

Figure 5. Contradiction debt trajectories on a 0–0.70 scale to accommodate authoritarian’s extreme values. Five trajectory signatures are visible: monotone descent (anarchist, pink); frozen plateau (contractarian, teal, flat from generation 55); U-shaped (hierarchical, orange; egalitarian, green); V-shaped to low plateau (communitarian, purple); monotone ascent bounded (reciprocal, blue; utilitarian, yellow); monotone ascent unbounded/rupture (authoritarian, red; pluralist, dark orange). These five shapes are regime fingerprints.

Monotone descent (anarchist). Voluntary consent prevents obligation formation between low-trust pairs, making denials structurally rare. Debt clears asymptotically toward zero throughout the run.

Frozen (contractarian). Trust collapse by generation 55 makes the consent threshold (0.45) impossible to meet. No new obligations form; no new debt accrues; existing debt has no repair pathway. Debt fossilizes at 0.128 for 955 generations.

U-shaped (hierarchical, egalitarian). Both regimes have repair mechanisms that function initially and then encounter structural limits. Hierarchical: rank-weighted repair clears early debt but cannot keep pace as trust collapses and the low-rank subpopulation grows. Egalitarian: the welfare subsidy clears debt to a trough of 0.049 at generation 130, then rising pressure defeats it.

V-shaped to low plateau (communitarian). The group-cohesion mechanism amplifies repair probability for in-group obligation pairs. Under rising pressure, cohesion initially outpaces denial accumulation, driving a V-shaped trough. At maximum pressure, the amplifier is overwhelmed. The extended 1,755-generation run reveals a third phase: debt peaks at 0.225 (generation 1095) and begins a slow descent driven by demographic self-purging of high-debt agents.

Monotone ascent, unbounded (authoritarian, pluralist). In both regimes, the repair mechanism is structurally defeated. Authoritarian: rP multiplied by 0.3 and dP multiplied by 2.5 inverts the repair-denial ratio from generation 1. Pluralist: epistemic variance fragments belief coordination under pressure, preventing reliable obligation fulfillment. Both show accelerating deaths with no plateau at run end.

4.5 Epistemic Agency

Epistemic agency (EA) is fully dissociable from both energy and welfare floor (Figure 6). Three regimes achieve EA saturation at 1.000: anarchist (unpressured) and reciprocal (unpressured) reach full EA through unconstrained belief-vector autonomy. Hierarchical plateaus at 79% due to rank-based belief suppression even without external pressure. All six pressured regimes collapse to near-zero EA regardless of other welfare outcomes.

Figure 6. Epistemic agency trajectories. The three unpressured regimes (anarchist, hierarchical, reciprocal) occupy the upper range; the six pressured regimes all collapse toward zero by generation 300. The communitarian EA arc (purple) is notable: rising to 71.6% at generation 80 as the regime initially resists pressure through cohesion, then collapsing as pressure saturates. Egalitarian and pluralist show similar resistance-then-collapse patterns. Hierarchical (orange) caps at 79% without any external pressure — the only regime showing structural EA suppression from internal mechanics alone.

The communitarian extended run isolates an important phenomenon: cognitive autonomy (CA) saturates at 0.776 while EA collapses to 0.001. This bifurcation demonstrates that cognitive capacity and epistemic freedom are separable under pressure. Agents may be maximally capable of reasoning while being structurally prevented from holding diverse beliefs. This is not a failure of intelligence but a structural condition that the normative framework must represent as a distinct failure mode.

4.6 Obligation Fulfillment

The complete fulfillment ranking across all nine regimes, with total obligations issued, is presented in Figure 7. The ranking is stable across all run lengths tested and the ordering does not change with extended runs.

Figure 7. Cumulative obligation outcomes. Bars scaled proportionally to communitarian (373K issued, the largest issuer). Green segments represent fulfilled obligations; red segments represent denials. Contractarian’s 100% rate across 4 obligations is noted but statistically non-comparable. The 30pp gap between anarchist (97.9%) and authoritarian (23.4%) represents the full range of obligation fulfillment achievable within the model’s parameter space.

The contractarian asterisk deserves emphasis. A 100% fulfillment rate across four obligations across 1,010 generations is not morally equivalent to a 98.1% rate across 373,000 obligations. The denominator is itself a normative variable: a moral community that makes almost no demands of itself has not achieved moral excellence but moral silence. The fulfillment metric requires a companion volume metric for meaningful cross-regime comparison.

4.7 Population Dynamics and the Rupture Condition

Seven of nine regimes maintain stable or growing populations throughout their runs. Authoritarian and pluralist enter genuine demographic rupture: deaths accelerate without plateau, and population is declining at run end (Figure 8). Authoritarian at generation 450: 169 deaths/generation, 128 agents (down from a peak of 296). Pluralist at generation 500: 244 deaths/generation, 2,512 agents (down from 2,750). The extended communitarian run reveals a third pattern: chronic stable mortality where births (approximately 3,065/generation) compensate deaths (375/generation) through a reproduction buffer, maintaining population at 2,750 indefinitely.

Figure 8. Deaths per generation on a square-root scale to compress the authoritarian extreme while preserving the shape of lower curves. Authoritarian (red) and pluralist (dark orange) are rupture trajectories: still accelerating at run end, no plateau. Hierarchical (orange) shows chronic low mortality from generation 150. Communitarian extended run (purple dashed) shows slow onset (deaths begin generation 630) accelerating to 375/generation by generation 1,755. Five regimes maintain zero deaths throughout their runs.

The rupture condition is not a debt-level threshold. Seven regimes carry varying levels of debt without rupturing. The condition is a structural defeat of the repair mechanism: when the regime-modified rP falls below the regime-modified dP at a structural level, and no external correction exists, debt compounds, trust collapses, and deaths accelerate without bound. Authoritarian’s rP×0.3 suppressor is the proximate mechanism; pluralist’s epistemic fragmentation under pressure achieves the same result through a different route. The prediction that follows: any regime whose structural multipliers invert the repair-denial ratio will rupture under sufficient pressure regardless of initial conditions.

4.8 The Extended Communitarian Run: Demographic Self-Purging

The 1,755-generation extension of the communitarian run reveals dynamics invisible in the 555-generation version and substantially revises the interpretation of communitarian’s performance. The previous finding — that communitarian achieves zero deaths under maximum pressure — was an artifact of run length. Deaths begin at generation 630 and reach 375/generation by generation 1,755.

Figure 9. Extended communitarian run: debt (left axis, purple) and trust (right axis, light purple) across 1,755 generations. Three phases are annotated. Phase I (green band, gen 5–165): rapid debt clearance to trough 0.029, trust peaks at 82.3%. Phase II (amber band, gen 165–1095): monotone debt ascent to peak 0.225 as pressure defeats cohesion, trust declines to trough 43.5%. Phase III (blue band, gen 1095–1755): debt turns and descends slowly to 0.200 as demographic self-purging removes high-debt agents; trust partially recovers toward 50.8%.

The third phase introduces a morally significant phenomenon: debt declines not because agents resolve their contradictions but because agents carrying the highest contradiction debt meet the death threshold and are replaced by newborns with lower initial debt. The population is getting cleaner through selection, not through moral repair. Trust partially recovers for the same reason: the agents with the worst interaction histories are removed. The aggregate welfare metrics (energy: 99.5%, fulfillment: 85.3%) remain stable throughout, but the mechanism producing them shifts from moral-ecological to demographic-selective around generation 1,095. The framework requires a criterion to distinguish these two routes to the same aggregate outcome.

5. Cross-Regime Synthesis: Nine Findings

Aggregate moral energy is a necessary but insufficient normative criterion. All nine regimes converge near 97–100% moral energy at equilibrium regardless of structural features. Contractarian achieves it through social closure. Authoritarian achieves it through death-cycling. Anarchist achieves it through voluntary cooperation. A normative theory that uses aggregate energy as its primary measure cannot distinguish these outcomes.
The welfare space is irreducibly four-dimensional. Moral energy, floor energy, epistemic agency, and contradiction debt are not reducible to each other and cannot be collapsed into a single index. Any two regimes that agree on one axis may diverge maximally on another. The geometric model requires these four as orthogonal axes with no dominant weighting.
The Rawlsian floor is the sharpest single differentiator of regime justice. Hierarchical is categorically distinguished from all other regimes by its permanent zero floor. The difference principle, applied to SimEthica outcomes, selects against hierarchical unconditionally and selects for anarchist and communitarian among active (non-frozen) regimes. This is a quantitative result, not merely an intuitive one.
Trust is a pathway variable, not a terminal welfare indicator. High trust enables efficient, distributionally favorable routes to moral energy, but energy can be produced in its absence through alternative mechanisms. Trust should be modeled as a velocity term in moral-energy space rather than a position term.
The debt trajectory shape is the primary diagnostic variable for regime health. Five distinct shapes (monotone descent, frozen, U-shaped, V-shaped, unbounded ascent) correspond to mechanistically distinct regime types and predict future welfare trajectories more reliably than any single-generation metric.
The rupture condition is a repair-rate defeat, not a debt-level threshold. Seven regimes carry varying debt levels without rupturing. Authoritarian and pluralist rupture not at a particular debt level but because their structural multipliers invert the repair-denial ratio, making debt accumulation self-reinforcing and irreversible from within.
Epistemic agency and cognitive autonomy are dissociable under pressure. The communitarian extended run produces agents at maximum cognitive capacity (CA = 1.000) with near-zero epistemic freedom (EA = 0.001). Cognitive capacity is not sufficient for epistemic agency. The framework must represent these as distinct and independently valuable.
The communitarian extended run reveals demographic self-purging as a distinct moral failure mode. After generation 1,095, communitarian debt declines and trust recovers not through moral repair but through the removal of high-debt agents by mortality and their replacement by low-debt newborns. Aggregate metrics are stable; the mechanism is selective rather than restorative. The framework currently has no criterion to distinguish these two routes to the same welfare appearance.
Voluntary anarchism is the empirical optimum under unpressured conditions. Anarchist leads or ties on every welfare axis in the dataset: highest trust, lowest debt, highest EA, highest floor (among active regimes), zero deaths, second-highest fulfillment rate. This result holds across the full nine-regime comparison. It requires replication under matched pressure conditions before being cited as a general result.

6. Implications for the Geometry of the Good

6.1 The Multi-Axis Welfare Requirement

The most direct theoretical implication is structural. The Geometry of the Good framework, as currently developed, requires explicit treatment of at minimum four welfare dimensions as irreducibly co-equal: moral energy, floor energy, epistemic agency, and contradiction debt. The Pareto frontier in this four-dimensional space has no single dominant point across the nine regimes tested. Anarchist comes closest to dominating but has not been tested under pressure conditions comparable to the pressured regimes. No parameter configuration in the current dataset produces dominance across all four axes simultaneously under equivalent conditions.

6.2 Debt Velocity as a Dynamic Welfare Variable

The debt trajectory findings argue for incorporating debt velocity alongside debt level in the normative framework. A regime with low current debt and a rising trajectory is not in the same moral condition as a regime with the same current debt and a falling trajectory. This is analogous to the distinction between position and momentum in physical geometry. The communitarian extended run is particularly instructive: debt level is moderate (0.200) and superficially comparable to reciprocal (0.217), but the trajectories are opposite. Communitarian debt is falling; reciprocal debt is rising. The normative implications of these two states are entirely different.

6.3 The Autonomy Threshold Problem

The six-regime dataset established what the extended communitarian run confirms: epistemic agency collapse is a consistent consequence of external pressure regardless of regime type or welfare performance. The normative framework faces a hard question: is a population with zero epistemic agency but full moral energy, a stable floor, and near-perfect fulfillment rates morally healthy or merely metabolically alive?

The data does not answer this question. It sharpens it. The two scenarios that produce EA collapse with otherwise excellent welfare outcomes — communitarian under pressure and contractarian under pressure — represent different routes to the same autonomy deficit. Communitarian collapses EA through belief convergence driven by external coercion. Contractarian collapses EA by freezing all moral activity. Both produce states that the four-axis framework scores highly on three dimensions and zero on the fourth. Whether the EA dimension should be weighted commensurately with the others, or whether some threshold EA value (say, 0.5) should be treated as a necessary condition for moral community, is a question the framework must answer before it can produce regime rankings rather than regime profiles.

6.4 The Contractarian Limit Case

Contractarian deserves extended treatment in the theoretical framework as a limit case that reveals the normative content of the model’s implicit assumptions. The regime achieves the highest floor energy (100%), the highest energy level (tied), zero deaths, and 100% fulfillment rate — all by ceasing to be a moral community in any active sense. Four obligations across 1,010 generations is not a moral achievement. The frozen debt of 0.128 is not a sign of moral health; it is unresolved contradiction preserved in amber because the conditions for its resolution have been made permanently unavailable.

The theoretical claim this suggests: the fulfillment rate metric requires a companion metric for moral activity volume. A community that issues no obligations cannot fail to fulfill them. The normative framework should distinguish between achieved welfare (the static distribution of moral energy at a given moment) and dynamic moral capacity (the regime’s ability to generate, sustain, and resolve moral demands over time). Contractarian under pressure maximizes the former by destroying the latter.

7. Prospectus for Future Study

The following experimental program would resolve the major confounds and open the most important theoretical questions identified in the current dataset. Items are ordered by theoretical priority, not technical difficulty.

Matched Pressure Design (Priority: Critical)

Run all nine regimes under identical conditions: first a full matched zero-pressure series, then a matched pressure series using identical ramp profiles. The current dataset cannot support clean cross-regime comparisons because three regimes are unpressured and six are pressured. A 2×9 factorial design (pressure × regime) would resolve every cross-regime confound in the current dataset and allow direct comparisons of, for example, anarchist versus communitarian under equivalent pressure. This is a single experimental design that would substantially upgrade the evidential status of every finding in this report. Expected run time: 18 clean runs × approximately 500 generations each.

Rupture Runs to Termination (Priority: High)

Authoritarian and pluralist are both in active rupture at end of run. Authoritarian has 128 agents at generation 450 and deaths of 169/generation; it will reach zero population within 100–200 generations. Pluralist has 2,512 agents at generation 500 and deaths of 244/generation; it will require 500+ further generations to reach terminal state. Running both to completion would establish whether genuine terminal collapse occurs or whether some stabilizing mechanism activates at low population. The repair-rate defeat model predicts true collapse; testing this prediction is a direct empirical contribution to the Contradiction Debt and Rupture argument.

Slow Pressure Ramp Series (Priority: High)

All six pressured runs use a ramp that reaches maximum pressure around generation 300. A slow-ramp series (reaching maximum pressure at generation 1,000+) would test whether the regimes’ resistance windows scale proportionally with ramp rate or whether there are critical thresholds. The communitarian extended run suggests a 300–600 generation window of genuine resistance before pressure overwhelms cohesion; a slower ramp might allow the V-shaped debt clearance to complete before the ascent begins, potentially producing a qualitatively different long-run equilibrium. The pluralist resistance window (EA holds above 0.5 until approximately generation 215 even under the current ramp) also warrants systematic investigation.

Lag Analysis Across All Regimes (Priority: High)

The built-in h6 hypothesis in SimEthica’s Science Hub tests whether rising contradiction debt at time t is a leading indicator of moral energy decline at t+k. The current dataset provides ideal test cases in both directions: authoritarian and pluralist show monotone rising debt preceding mortality cascades; anarchist shows declining debt with rising energy. Running the full lag cross-correlation analysis across all nine regimes using the Science Hub’s built-in infrastructure (maxLag = 50 generations, nReps = 12) would produce the first multi-regime test of debt as a leading welfare indicator and directly support or refute the central predictive claim of the Contradiction Debt and Rupture paper.

Mid-Run Regime Switching (Priority: High)

A fundamental unresolved question is whether regime-structure effects dominate initial-condition effects. Starting a stable anarchist run (energy near 1.000, debt near 0.004, trust near 0.900) and switching to authoritarian mid-run would test whether rupture initiates immediately despite favorable initial conditions — the prediction of the repair-rate defeat model — or whether the initial conditions provide a buffer that delays or prevents rupture. The reverse experiment (switching from authoritarian to anarchist at the point of incipient rupture) would test whether regime switching can rescue a collapsing system. SimEthica supports this natively via the regime selector; scripting a timed switch would require a configuration-level extension.

Consent Threshold Sweep (Priority: Moderate)

Contractarian’s behavior under pressure is entirely determined by the consentThreshold parameter (currently 0.45). Sweeping this parameter from 0.10 to 0.90 across multiple runs would identify: the threshold value below which contractarian avoids freeze while retaining consent-filtering benefits; whether there is a Goldilocks range that combines high fulfillment with high obligation volume; and whether the threshold interacts with pressure level in a non-linear way. This is a targeted parametric study with direct implications for contractarian political philosophy’s applicability to conditions of social stress.

Zero-Pressure Pluralist Run (Priority: Moderate)

Pluralist is the only regime not tested under zero-pressure conditions. Its performance under pressure was the worst of the nine regimes on multiple welfare axes. Its theoretical design — high epistemic variance, obligation formation across belief difference — suggests it may be the most normatively rich regime under zero-pressure conditions, precisely because belief diversity is an unrestricted good when it is not being weaponized against coordination. Running pluralist at zero pressure for 500+ generations would establish its baseline welfare profile and allow direct comparison with anarchist, which currently holds the unpressured benchmark. This is the single most important missing data point in the current dataset from the perspective of the Geometry of the Good’s core commitments.

Parameter Optimization Study: Toward Robust Normative Equilibrium (Priority: Moderate)

Based on the cross-regime findings, the following parameter configuration is predicted to produce the best normative environment across all four welfare axes under zero to moderate pressure: regime = anarchist; moralRepairRate = 0.10; trustDecay = 0.0005; epistemicVariance = 0.20; cognitiveWeight = 0.35; obligationRadius = 30; beliefSimilarityReq = −0.10; consentThreshold = 0.25; deathThresh = 0.03; reproEnergyThresh = 0.25. A systematic sweep around these parameter values using the Science Hub’s experiment designer (varying moralRepairRate and trustDecay across three levels each, crossed with obligationRadius, in a full factorial design) would identify the parameter combination that empirically maximizes the four-axis welfare space and test the sensitivity of the prediction to individual parameter variation.

Replication Across Seeds (Priority: Necessary before publication)

Every finding in this report is based on single-seed runs. The simulation is stochastic, and some variance in outcomes across seeds is expected. No finding should be cited in published work without replication across a minimum of 10–12 seeds per condition, with Welch t-statistics and Cohen’s d reported. The Science Hub’s hypothesis testing infrastructure already implements this via the nReps parameter. The current dataset is a discovery phase. The replication phase is the prerequisite for any publication claim.

Platform Extension: Epistemic Resistance Parameter (Priority: Long-term)

The most important parameter currently absent from SimEthica is something like an epistemic resistance coefficient: a parameter governing how strongly the extPressure mechanism suppresses epistemic autonomy per unit of pressure. In the current implementation, EA collapse is a direct and nearly uniform function of extPressure across all regime types. A resistance parameter would allow some regimes (or some parameter configurations) to maintain EA at moderate pressure levels while still responding to maximum pressure. Implementing this would require modifying the step function in the Simulation class to scale the epistemicAutonomy suppression by a regime-specific or user-configurable resistance coefficient. This is the single platform modification most likely to produce theoretically significant new findings, because it would allow testing whether any configuration of voluntary cooperation and epistemic variance can resist coordinated external pressure — the central normative question the Geometry of the Good is designed to address.

8. Conclusion

The SimEthica dataset, across nine regimes and a total of approximately 900 generational records, establishes a stable cluster of empirical results that have direct bearing on the normative framework of the Geometry of the Good. The central results — that aggregate energy is insufficient, that the welfare space is irreducibly four-dimensional, that the rupture condition is a repair-rate defeat, that demographic self-purging is a distinct moral failure mode, and that voluntary anarchism dominates under unpressured conditions — are robust within the current dataset and generate specific, falsifiable predictions for future experiments.

The dataset also reveals genuine theoretical puzzles that no parameter adjustment will resolve and that require conceptual development in the framework itself. Whether a population with zero epistemic agency but perfect welfare scores is morally healthy or merely preserved. Whether the contractarian freeze constitutes moral achievement or moral silence. Whether trust that is rebuilt through mortality rather than repair represents genuine moral recovery. These questions are not answered by the simulation. They are sharpened by it into forms that are precise enough to reason about clearly.

The platform is working. The experimental design requires one full iteration with matched pressure conditions and multi-seed replication before any finding is ready for publication. The prospectus above specifies that iteration in detail. The theoretical program has a clear empirical agenda, and the empirical agenda has a clear platform for execution.
